## üåø What is Direct Air Capture (DAC)?

Direct Air Capture refers to a technology that uses mechanical systems‚Äîsuch as large fans and chemical reactions‚Äîto pull CO‚ÇÇ from the atmosphere. Once captured, this CO‚ÇÇ can either be stored underground permanently or reused in industrial applications, such as making concrete or synthetic fuels.

## üîç How Does Direct Air Capture Work?

The process of DAC involves several steps, which include **air intake**, **chemical capture**, and **CO‚ÇÇ concentration and compression**. Here's a more detailed breakdown:

1. **Air Intake**: DAC facilities use large fans to pull air into the system. This is crucial because atmospheric CO‚ÇÇ is present in relatively low concentrations (~0.04%), so capturing it requires processing massive volumes of air.

2. **Chemical Reaction**: As the air passes through the system, it interacts with chemicals (either liquid solvents or solid filters) that bind with CO‚ÇÇ molecules. These materials are specifically designed to capture and hold onto CO‚ÇÇ while letting other gases pass through.

3. **CO‚ÇÇ Release**: Once the CO‚ÇÇ is captured, the chemical reaction is reversed through heat or pressure, allowing the CO‚ÇÇ to be released in a concentrated form. The resulting CO‚ÇÇ is then ready for either storage or use.

4. **Storage or Utilization**: Captured CO‚ÇÇ can be injected underground in geological formations for long-term storage (known as carbon sequestration) or used in industries like concrete production, which can help reduce emissions from those sectors.

## üí° Resource Considerations for DAC

While DAC is promising, it‚Äôs important to consider the resources required to operate these systems at scale. According to the [World Resources Institute](https://www.wri.org/insights/direct-air-capture-resource-considerations-and-costs-carbon-removal#:~:text=Direct%20air%20capture%20is%20a,release%20carbon%20into%20the%20atmosphere.), DAC systems require substantial amounts of energy, water, and land. Here‚Äôs a look at some of the key resource needs:

### 1. **Energy Demand**
DAC systems are energy-intensive, especially in the stages where captured CO‚ÇÇ needs to be released and compressed. The WRI article notes that **direct air capture facilities require 8 to 12 gigajoules (GJ) of energy per ton of CO‚ÇÇ captured**, depending on the technology used. For context, 1 GJ is equivalent to the energy needed to power a home for about two weeks.

Since DAC requires so much energy, it‚Äôs crucial that this energy comes from **renewable sources** like wind or solar to ensure the overall process remains carbon neutral. If fossil fuels were used to power DAC plants, they could end up emitting more CO‚ÇÇ than they remove.

### 2. **Water Use**
DAC also demands water, particularly in **liquid solvent systems**, which use water as part of the chemical reactions needed to capture CO‚ÇÇ. According to the WRI, DAC systems could require anywhere from **1 to 7 tons of water per ton of CO‚ÇÇ removed**.

Given that water scarcity is already a challenge in many parts of the world, the large water requirements of DAC could limit where these plants are built or necessitate innovations in **water recycling** within DAC facilities.

### 3. **Land Footprint**
The land required for DAC facilities is comparatively small when measured against other carbon removal methods, like afforestation or bioenergy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS). However, as DAC scales, there may still be competition for land use in areas where DAC could be most effective, such as regions with abundant renewable energy resources for powering these plants.

## üí∞ What Does DAC Cost?

The cost of DAC is one of the major barriers to scaling it up for widespread use. Current estimates suggest that capturing a ton of CO‚ÇÇ using DAC costs between **$250 and $600**. However, these costs are expected to drop as the technology improves, plants scale up, and more investments are made in the sector.

**Key Factors Influencing DAC Costs**:
- **Energy Prices**: The cost of the electricity and heat required to run DAC systems is one of the largest contributors to overall costs.
- **Technology Advancements**: As with most new technologies, costs will likely decrease as DAC systems become more efficient and mass-produced.
- **Carbon Markets**: Government policies that put a price on carbon emissions (such as carbon taxes or emissions trading systems) could create a financial incentive for DAC by making it more profitable to capture and store CO‚ÇÇ.
